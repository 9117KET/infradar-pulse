# Customer Discovery Playbook — InfraRadar Pulse

A practical guide for running customer conversations that generate signal, not validation.
Applies The Mom Test framework directly to InfraRadar's product, personas, and go-to-market stage.

---

## The Core Rule

**Ask about their life, not your product.**

Every question you want to ask ("Would you use InfraRadar?", "What would you pay for this?") is useless.
Every question about their current behavior, past frustrations, and existing spend is gold.

If your mom could answer yes without lying, the question is wrong.

---

## Who to Talk To First

Not every person with the problem is worth your time right now. Prioritize in this order:

### Tier 1 - Talk to immediately
People who already spend money trying to solve the infrastructure intelligence problem:

- **DFI Analysts** at World Bank, IFC, ADB, AfDB, EBRD regional offices who manage project portfolios manually
- **Project Finance Directors** at infrastructure banks or PE funds actively deploying capital
- **BD Directors at EPC contractors** (Bechtel, Vinci, Bouygues, local tier-1 firms) tracking tender pipelines
- **Infrastructure Analysts at investment managers** (Brookfield, Macquarie, GIP) who pay for GlobalData or Wood Mackenzie

These people already have a line item for infrastructure intelligence. They are not hypothetical buyers.

### Tier 2 - Talk to after Tier 1 confirms the problem
- Senior consultants at advisory firms (Deloitte Infrastructure, KPMG Projects) who produce the expensive PDFs
- Government BD teams at export credit agencies (UKEF, US EXIM, Euler Hermes) tracking deal flow
- Insurance underwriters pricing political risk and construction delay

### Tier 3 - Interesting but not yet
- Junior analysts (they feel the pain but don't control the budget)
- Strategy teams at companies not yet active in infrastructure
- Academic researchers (will never pay)

**Who controls the budget?** For infrastructure intelligence, the decision sits with: Director/VP of Business Development, Head of Project Finance, CIO/Research Director. Map that person before every conversation.

## The Conversation Structure

### Before the meeting - write this down
1. What three things would change your product roadmap if you learned them?
2. What would you have to hear to seriously reconsider the business model?
3. What commitment would you ask for if the conversation goes well?

If you cannot answer these before the meeting, you are going to pitch, not learn.

### The opening (do not mention your product for the first 15 minutes)
"I'm researching how teams like yours track infrastructure projects and opportunities. I'm not here to pitch anything - I want to understand how this actually works day to day. Would it be okay if I asked you a few questions about your process?"

### The questions that actually work

#### On current workflow
- "Walk me through the last time you needed to assess a specific project before a bid or investment decision. What did you actually do?"
- "Where does the information come from? Who pulls it? How long does it take?"
- "What tools or subscriptions does your team currently use for project intelligence?"
- "When did you last export a report or briefing from one of those tools? What did it contain?"

#### On pain and cost
- "What is the single most frustrating part of the process you just described?"
- "Has your team ever made a decision that turned out to be wrong because the intelligence was late or incomplete? What happened?"
- "How much does your team spend annually on research subscriptions, data vendors, or consultant engagements for infrastructure intelligence?"
- "If you could cut that by half but get better coverage, what would stop you from switching?"

#### On workarounds (this is where the real signal is)
- "What do people on your team do when they can't find reliable data on a project in, say, West Africa or Central Asia?"
- "Have you ever tried to build an internal tracker or dashboard? What happened to it?"
- "Are there regions or project stages where you just accept that your information will be incomplete?"

#### On procurement and decision-making
- "If something like InfraRadar existed today, who at your organization would need to approve purchasing it?"
- "What does your current vendor evaluation process look like?"
- "What would a pilot need to demonstrate for your team to want to sign an annual contract?"

### Questions to avoid completely
- "Would you use a platform that does X?" - hypothetical, always yes
- "How much would you pay for this?" - hypothetical, reveals nothing
- "Do you think there's a market for this?" - you are asking them to be an analyst
- "What features would you want?" - they will describe their current tool with minor improvements

## What to Listen For (Signal vs. Noise)

### This is signal - write it down immediately
- They describe a specific past incident where bad/missing data cost them money or a deal
- They name a specific tool they pay for and complain about it
- They pull up their screen to show you how they actually work
- They mention a colleague or team who has the same problem
- They ask "when can I try this?" or "how do I sign up?"
- They introduce you to someone else in the meeting who has the budget
- They say something like "we're currently paying €X/month for [competitor] and it's still not enough"

### This is noise - note it but do not act on it
- "This is really impressive"
- "I could definitely see myself using this"
- "The market really needs something like this"
- "Have you talked to [famous fund name]? They'd love this"
- Any statement about what they "would" do in the future

Compliments feel good and contain no information.

### The commitment test
Before every meeting ends, push for one of these:

| Commitment type | What it sounds like | What it proves |
|---|---|---|
| **Money** | "Send me a quote for a pilot" or "what's your enterprise pricing?" | Willingness to pay exists |
| **Time** | "Can you set up a demo with my research director next week?" | Internal champion willing to spend political capital |
| **Reputation** | "I'll introduce you to the BD lead at [competitor/partner firm]" | Believes in it enough to put their name on it |
| **Data** | "Send me your NDA, I'll share our current process doc so you can map against it" | Genuine problem-solution fit conversation |

"Keep me posted" = zero commitment. End the meeting, follow up with specifics.

## InfraRadar-Specific Discovery Questions

These are tailored to the product's actual feature set. Use these to validate specific bets:

### Portfolio tracking and alerts
- "If a project you were monitoring hit a delay signal 6 months before it became public - how much would that change your decision-making?"
- "Right now, how do you find out when a project you care about changes stage, status, or contractor?"
- "Do you currently set up Google Alerts or RSS feeds for projects? Why or why not?"

### Satellite verification feature
- "Have you ever had a project that looked fine on paper but the construction had clearly stalled? How did you find out?"
- "Does your team use satellite imagery for any purpose today? What provider?"
- "Would you trust a confidence score on construction progress if it was derived from satellite analysis? What would make you trust or distrust it?"

### AI research agent
- "When a junior analyst researches a project from scratch, what does that take? Hours? Days?"
- "Have you experimented with any LLM tools for research? What worked, what didn't?"
- "If you could get a structured brief on any project in any region within 10 minutes, what would you use it for first?"

### Pricing and tier structure
- "What is the smallest team at your organization that would use a tool like this?"
- "Do you currently pay per-seat for data tools, or is it a flat enterprise license?"
- "What is your budget cycle for research tools? When do you evaluate vendors?"

### The MDB data angle (World Bank, IFC, ADB, AfDB, EBRD)
- "Do you currently pull data directly from MDB pipelines? How?"
- "Is there value in having MDB project data cross-referenced with satellite and news, or is the raw MDB data sufficient for your purposes?"
- "Which development banks are most relevant to your portfolio regions?"

## Persona-Specific Conversation Guides

### DFI Analyst
**Their world:** Manage a portfolio of 50-200 active projects across multiple countries. Spend time in Excel, SharePoint, and occasional GlobalData/Wood Mackenzie reports. Frustrated by how fast situations change between quarterly briefings.

**Best opening question:** "Walk me through how you monitor a project after it moves from approval to construction. What does your regular review process look like?"

**Listen for:** Manual monitoring workflows, Excel or SharePoint trackers, reliance on in-country officers for updates

**Strongest signal:** They describe a project that had a problem they learned about late, and money or reputation was affected

### Project Finance Director / BD Lead at EPC
**Their world:** Chasing 20-30 active tenders at once across multiple regions. Competitive intelligence is everything. Currently paying for tender aggregators (Tendersinfo, DG Market, MEED) and getting incomplete coverage.

**Best opening question:** "What does your team's process look like from 'we heard about a tender' to 'we submit a bid'? Where does the intelligence work happen?"

**Listen for:** How many people work on bid intelligence, how much time it takes, what they miss

**Strongest signal:** "We lost a bid because we found out too late" or "we can't cover Southeast Asia / West Africa properly"

### Infrastructure Investor (PE / Asset Manager)
**Their world:** Deal sourcing and portfolio monitoring. Pays for Wood Mackenzie or GlobalData at significant cost. Frustrated by lack of emerging market coverage and static quarterly reports.

**Best opening question:** "When you're evaluating a greenfield project in [target region], what does your due diligence process look like for the project itself - separate from the financial model?"

**Listen for:** What they currently pay, gaps in emerging market coverage, how they verify contractor track records

**Strongest signal:** They mention a specific region or sector where data is bad, and they still have exposure there

## After Every Conversation

Write these down within 30 minutes while memory is fresh:

1. **Facts learned** - specific numbers, tools, processes, spend they mentioned
2. **Signals** - commitment language, specific pain moments they described
3. **Noise** - compliments and vague enthusiasm to discount
4. **What changed** - any assumption about the product or model this conversation challenged
5. **Next step** - what did you commit to, what did they commit to

If you cannot fill in "What changed," the conversation produced no learning.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Pitching instead of learning
If you are talking more than 30% of the time in the first meeting, stop. You are pitching. Ask another question.

### Fishing for compliments
"So you think the satellite verification angle is interesting?" is not a question. It is asking them to validate your assumption. They will say yes.

### Taking feature requests at face value
When someone says "I'd want it to also do X," the correct response is: "Tell me about a time when not having X caused you a problem." If they can't describe a real incident, X is not a priority.

### Mistaking enthusiasm for intent
Infrastructure decision cycles are long. Enthusiasm in November means nothing if the budget cycle is January. Ask when they evaluate tools, who signs off, and what the next step is - always.

### Talking to the wrong people
Junior analysts feel the pain but do not control the budget. Talking to 10 junior analysts tells you what the product should look like; it does not tell you who will pay for it. Map the buyer before every conversation.

Log every conversation in a simple format:

```

What they said that was noise:
-

Commitment made (by them):

Next step + deadline:
```

When you have 10-15 conversations logged, look for the pattern: which pain comes up repeatedly, which feature generates commitment language, which persona has budget vs. just interest.

**Target before going public:** 20 conversations. At least 10 with Tier 1 personas. At least 3 commitments (LOI, paid pilot, or intro to budget holder).

## Pilot Design

When the conversation goes well and they want to try it, do not give free access and wait.
Structure a pilot that generates learning:

- **Duration**: 30 days
- **Success criteria**: Agree on what "working" looks like before they start ("you will have tracked X projects and run Y research queries by day 30")
- **Check-in**: Midpoint call (day 15) to hear what they are actually using
- **End review**: Structured debrief using the same questions from this guide
- **Ask for**: A written statement of what changed in their workflow (use this as a case study with permission)
- **Price it**: Even a token amount ($1) confirms intent. Free pilots generate polite feedback, not signal.

## What Builds the Best Product

Based on the platform's current state and the Mom Test framework, the highest-value things to learn from customers are:

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Which alert category (political, financial, regulatory, supply chain, construction) causes the most real-world decisions? | Focus AI agent cycles and UI on the highest-signal category |
| Do buyers prefer coverage breadth (14 regions) or depth (5 regions, more verified)? | Shapes the MDB ingest prioritization |
| Is satellite verification a differentiator or a gimmick to this audience? | If nobody asks about it unprompted, deprioritize it |
| Do teams want individual seat access or institutional data access (API/webhook)? | Shapes whether to invest in the Enterprise tier or the self-serve tiers first |
| What does the first 10 minutes of value look like? | Design the onboarding around the specific moment they feel "this works" |
| What format do they use to share intelligence internally? | PDF with watermarks, or raw data exports, or Slack/Teams integration? |

The answers to these questions are not in the codebase. They are in the conversations.
