## Part 1 - The Industry, From First Principles

---

### 1.1 What Infrastructure Actually Is

Infrastructure is the physical foundation that makes economic activity possible. It is not a product you manufacture and sell - it is a capability that a country, region, or community installs once and uses for decades.

There are two categories:

**Hard infrastructure** - physical assets:
- Transport: roads, highways, bridges, tunnels, railways, airports, seaports, dryports (inland container depots)
- Energy: power plants, transmission lines, pipelines, storage tanks, substations
- Water: dams, treatment plants, distribution networks, wastewater systems
- Digital: undersea cables, data centers, telecoms towers, fiber networks
- Industrial: cement plants, steel mills, refineries, fertilizer factories
- Mining: extraction facilities, processing plants, haul roads

**Soft infrastructure** - systems that support hard infrastructure:
- Regulatory frameworks (building codes, environmental permits)
- Financial systems (project bonds, loan guarantees)
- Governance structures (concession agreements, PPP legislation)

**Why this matters in sales:** When a prospect talks about "tracking infrastructure," they mean the hard assets. When they talk about "risk," they often mean the soft infrastructure failing - the permit gets revoked, the government changes, the regulator blocks the project. InfraRadar tracks both.

### 1.2 Who Builds Infrastructure and Why It Is Complicated

Building a major infrastructure project involves at least five different types of organizations, each with different incentives:

**Owners / Sponsors**
The entity that ends up owning the asset. Could be a government ministry, a state-owned enterprise (SOE), a private investor, or a special purpose vehicle (SPV). They commission the project, secure the land, obtain permits, and ultimately sign the main contracts. Their goal is to have a working asset at the lowest possible lifecycle cost.

**EPC Contractors (Engineering, Procurement, Construction)**
The firms that actually build things. EPC means one contractor takes full responsibility for all three phases: designing the engineering, procuring the materials and equipment, and managing the physical construction. This is a lump-sum arrangement - the contractor takes the risk that the project finishes on budget and on time.

Examples: Bechtel, Vinci, Bouygues, Saipem, Consolidated Contractors Company (CCC), POSCO E&C, China State Construction, Larsen & Toubro, Julius Berger.

EPC contractors are a primary buyer of InfraRadar because their entire business is finding and winning these projects before competitors do. They need to know which projects are entering the tender stage 6-12 months before the deadline so they can prepare bids.

**Sub-contractors and Suppliers**
EPC contractors do not do everything themselves. They subcontract civil works, electrical systems, mechanical equipment, and specialist services. Suppliers provide cement, steel, pipes, transformers. These companies also benefit from intelligence about what is being built and when.

**Financiers**
Infrastructure is capital intensive. A $500M port project cannot be funded from cash flow. Financing comes from:
- Development Finance Institutions (DFIs) and Multilateral Development Banks (MDBs) - see section 1.4
- Commercial banks (project finance loans)
- Institutional investors (infrastructure funds, pension funds)
- Export credit agencies (ECAs) - government-backed lenders that finance contracts won by their country's exporters
- Bonds (project bonds, green bonds, sukuk in Islamic finance markets)

**Consultants and Advisors**
Every major project has a constellation of advisors: legal firms (structuring contracts and concession agreements), financial advisors (arranging the debt and equity), technical advisors (reviewing engineering designs), and market consultants (validating demand forecasts). These firms also use infrastructure intelligence to identify business development opportunities.

### 1.3 The Project Lifecycle - What Each Stage Actually Means

This is the sequence of stages every infrastructure project goes through. Understanding this is critical because InfraRadar tracks and displays projects at each stage, and each stage means something different to each buyer type.

**Planned**
The project exists as an intention. A government has included it in a national development plan (NDP), a feasibility study has been commissioned, or a donor has expressed interest in funding it. At this stage there is no procurement, no contractor, and often no finalized design. The information is usually sparse - a project name, a sector, a rough value estimate, a target country.

*Who cares about this stage:* EPC contractors who want maximum lead time to position themselves. Investors scouting pipeline opportunities. Advisory firms looking to pitch feasibility study mandates.

*InfraRadar advantage:* MDB pipeline data (World Bank, IFC, ADB, AfDB, EBRD) gives us planned-stage projects months or years before they become public tenders. Most competitors only track from tender stage onwards.

**Tender**
The owner has decided to build the project and is formally inviting contractors to submit bids. This is also called procurement. A tender package (also called an ITT - Invitation to Tender, or RFP - Request for Proposals) is released containing the technical scope, contract terms, and bid submission deadline.

*Who cares about this stage:* EPC contractors (they need to submit a bid), suppliers (they need to quote to the EPC teams), advisors (they track awards to understand competitive dynamics).

*Key sub-events in the tender stage tracked by InfraRadar:*
- Tender Open: the procurement officially opens for bids
- Tender Closed: bid submission deadline passes
- Award: a winner is announced
- Cancellation: the tender is withdrawn (a major signal - usually means financial trouble, political change, or project redesign)
- Re-Tender: the tender was cancelled and restarted (often after a failed first round)
- Dispute: a losing bidder challenges the outcome
- Arbitration: the dispute escalates to formal arbitration

**Awarded**
A contractor has been selected. The contract has been signed (or is being finalized). This is one of the highest-value data points in the industry because it tells competitors who won, what price, and therefore what benchmarks exist for similar scopes.

*Who cares about this stage:* Competitors analyzing win rates. Financiers beginning to structure the project finance package. Sub-contractors pitching the winning EPC team.

**Financing**
Separate from contract award. The owner needs to arrange the debt and equity package before construction can start. This phase can take 6 months to 3 years on large projects. It involves negotiating loan agreements, obtaining guarantees, completing environmental and social impact assessments (ESIA) required by lenders, and satisfying all conditions precedent.

*Why this stage matters:* Many projects that have been awarded never reach construction because financing falls through. If InfraRadar detects that a project has been awarded but is stuck in financing for 18+ months, that is a risk signal.

**Construction**
Physical work on site has begun. This stage can last 2-10 years depending on project scale. Key events during construction: ground-breaking ceremony (ceremonial start), first concrete pour, structural completion, mechanical completion, commissioning, and commercial operations date (COD).

*Who cares about this stage:* Insurance underwriters pricing construction-phase risk. Investors monitoring their capital deployment. Supply chain and logistics companies planning material delivery. Satellite imagery is the most reliable independent monitor of construction progress - a project showing no ground disturbance for 6 months despite claiming to be "on schedule" is a flag.

**Completed**
The asset is operational and has reached commercial operations date (COD). The construction contract is closed out. For EPC contractors, this is when retention money is released and the defects liability period begins.

*Why this matters to InfraRadar users:* Completed projects become comparables. They inform what the next project in the same region and sector will cost, how long it will take, and which contractors have a track record.

**Stopped / Cancelled**
The project has been indefinitely suspended or permanently cancelled. This is a critical signal. Reasons include: political change (new government cancels predecessor's projects), funding collapse, contractor insolvency, community opposition, environmental permit denial, or sovereign debt crisis. These are tracked in InfraRadar as anomaly signals.

### 1.4 MDBs and DFIs - The Backbone of Emerging Market Infrastructure

This is one of the most important sections for InfraRadar sales conversations. The majority of infrastructure projects in Africa, Southeast Asia, South Asia, Central Asia, and the Pacific are co-financed by Multilateral Development Banks.

**What a Multilateral Development Bank (MDB) is**
An MDB is an international financial institution owned by its member governments. Its purpose is to lend to developing countries for projects that the private market will not fully finance because the risk is too high, the return horizon too long, or the country too poor to access capital markets. MDB loans come at concessional rates (below market) and with long tenors (20-40 years).

The five MDBs whose data InfraRadar ingests:

**World Bank Group (WBG)**
The largest and most global. Its lending arm, the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD), lends to middle-income countries. The International Development Association (IDA) lends to the poorest countries on highly concessional terms. The World Bank publishes its full project pipeline publicly. This is one of InfraRadar's core data sources.
Headquarters: Washington D.C.
Focus regions: Global
Annual commitments: ~$70B+

**IFC (International Finance Corporation)**
The private sector arm of the World Bank Group. IFC invests directly in private sector projects - it takes equity, provides loans, and offers guarantees. IFC is critical because it de-risks private investment in emerging markets. When IFC invests in a project, it signals credibility and often unlocks commercial bank financing.
Focus: Private sector projects in emerging markets
What InfraRadar pulls: Active and pipeline private sector projects IFC has committed to

**ADB (Asian Development Bank)**
Focused exclusively on Asia and the Pacific. Finances sovereign (government) and non-sovereign (private sector) projects across 68 member countries. Particularly active in Southeast Asia, South Asia, Central Asia, and the Pacific Islands.
Headquarters: Manila, Philippines
Annual commitments: ~$20-30B

**AfDB (African Development Bank)**
Focused on Africa. Finances projects across 54 African member countries. Particularly active in power, transport, water, and agriculture. Publishes a detailed project portal with procurement notices.
Headquarters: Abidjan, Côte d'Ivoire
Annual commitments: ~$10-15B

**EBRD (European Bank for Reconstruction and Development)**
Focused on Central and Eastern Europe, Central Asia, the Southern and Eastern Mediterranean, and sub-Saharan Africa (recently expanded mandate). Unique in that it only invests in countries committed to market-economy principles. Active in private sector deals, energy transition, and green infrastructure.
Headquarters: London
Annual commitments: ~$13-15B

**Why MDB data is so valuable**
MBDs publish their project pipeline before projects go to public tender. A World Bank project in its "pipeline" is typically 12-36 months away from procurement. This gives EPC contractors, suppliers, and advisors an enormous head start. Before InfraRadar, tracking MDB pipelines required manually checking five different portals, each with a different format, updated at different frequencies. InfraRadar ingests all five daily and cross-references them with news, regulatory filings, and satellite data.

**What a DFI is vs. an MDB**
Development Finance Institution (DFI) is the broader category. MDBs are multilateral (owned by many governments). DFIs can also be bilateral - owned by a single government to finance development projects in other countries. Examples:
- CDC Group / British International Investment (UK)
- DEG (Germany)
- FMO (Netherlands)
- Proparco (France)
- DFC (US International Development Finance Corporation)
- OPIC (predecessor to DFC)

DFI analysts at these institutions are a primary persona for InfraRadar. They manage portfolios of 50-300+ projects and need continuous monitoring.

### 1.5 Procurement and Tenders - How Infrastructure Is Bought

Understanding procurement is essential for credibility with EPC and BD (business development) audiences.

**Types of procurement procedures**

**International Competitive Bidding (ICB)**
Open to all qualified contractors globally. Required by MDBs for contracts above threshold values. The most transparent, most competitive, and most useful to InfraRadar users because the information is publicly disclosed.

**National Competitive Bidding (NCB)**
Open only to contractors registered in the country. Used for smaller or strategically sensitive contracts. Often less publicly visible.

**Direct Contracting / Sole Source**
No competition. The owner selects a contractor directly. This happens for emergency repairs, proprietary technology, or politically connected contracts. Always a risk signal when it appears where ICB should be used.

**Framework Agreements**
A pre-qualified list of contractors from which the owner draws for multiple contracts over a period. Common in road maintenance and utility works.

**The tender documents - what they contain**
When InfraRadar tracks a tender event, these are the documents behind it:
- **Instructions to Bidders (ITB)**: rules for submitting the bid
- **Bid Data Sheet (BDS)**: specific numbers and dates for this tender
- **Evaluation Criteria**: how bids will be scored (usually: pass/fail technical, then lowest price)
- **General Conditions of Contract (GCC)**: standard legal terms (often FIDIC)
- **Particular Conditions of Contract (PCC)**: project-specific modifications to standard terms
- **Technical Specifications**: what must be built and to what standard
- **Bill of Quantities (BoQ)**: itemized list of work items and quantities for pricing
- **Employer's Requirements (for design-build)**: performance outcomes rather than prescriptive design

**FIDIC Contracts**
FIDIC (Fédération Internationale des Ingénieurs-Conseils) is the international body that publishes standardized construction contract templates. When someone says "this is a FIDIC Red Book contract," they mean it uses FIDIC's standard terms for construction (employer-designed). The "Yellow Book" is for EPC/design-build. The "Silver Book" is for full turnkey EPC. Understanding which contract type applies tells you who takes the design risk.

**Retention money**
In most construction contracts, the owner withholds 5-10% of each payment as retention until the project is substantially complete. Half is released at completion; the other half is released at the end of the Defects Liability Period (typically 12-24 months). This is why contractor financial health matters - they are owed money for months or years and need working capital bridges.

### 1.6 Project Finance - How Large Infrastructure Is Funded

**What project finance is**
Project finance is a lending structure where repayment comes entirely from the revenues generated by the project itself - not from the balance sheet of the sponsor. The project is structured as a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) that holds the assets and contracts. Lenders have a claim only on the SPV, not on the parent companies behind it.

This is different from corporate finance, where a company borrows against its total assets and creditworthiness.

**Why project finance matters for InfraRadar**
Project finance structures are publicly disclosed (to varying degrees). They reveal: who the lenders are, what the loan amounts are, what the security package looks like, and what the key risk allocations are. When InfraRadar's Funding Tracker agent detects a change in a project's financing structure - a lender withdrawing, a guarantee expiring, a refinancing - that is a significant risk signal.

**Key project finance concepts**

*Senior debt:* The primary loans. Repaid first. Secured against the project's assets and cash flows. Typically 60-80% of total project cost.

*Mezzanine debt:* A layer between senior debt and equity. Higher risk than senior, lower risk than equity. Used to fill gaps in the capital structure.

*Equity:* The owners' capital. Takes the most risk (repaid last) but gets the upside. Typically 20-40% of total project cost.

*Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR):* The ratio of the project's cash flow to its debt service payments (principal + interest). Lenders require a minimum DSCR (typically 1.2-1.4x). If projected revenues fall, DSCR falls, and the project may breach its loan covenants.

*Conditions Precedent (CPs):* Conditions that must be satisfied before lenders disburse funds. Common CPs: finalized construction contract, insurance policies in place, government permits obtained, environmental and social action plan approved.

*Financial Close:* The moment all CPs are satisfied and the loan agreements are signed. Projects frequently stall between award and financial close. An award that has not reached financial close in 24+ months is an anomaly signal in InfraRadar.

*Off-take agreement:* A contract where a buyer commits to purchase the project's output (electricity, water, processed minerals) at a fixed price for a fixed term. This is what makes the project's revenue predictable enough for lenders to lend. For a power plant, this is called a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA). For a port, it might be a minimum throughput guarantee.

*Concession agreement:* A government-to-private-company agreement that grants the right to build, operate, and charge for a public service (a toll road, a port, an airport) for a fixed period (typically 20-35 years), after which the asset reverts to the government. This is a BOT structure.

*BOT (Build-Operate-Transfer):* The private sector builds the asset, operates it and recovers its investment through user fees, then transfers it to the government. Variants include BOOT (own before transfer), BOO (build-own-operate, no transfer), DBFM (design-build-finance-maintain), DBFMO (design-build-finance-maintain-operate).

*PPP (Public-Private Partnership):* The broad category covering any arrangement where government and private sector share infrastructure risk and return. BOT is one type. Others include availability payment models (government pays based on availability rather than usage) and hybrid capital structures.

### 1.7 Risk in Infrastructure - The Nine Dimensions InfraRadar Tracks

Infrastructure projects face risk across nine categories. Each has a distinct cause, impact, and monitoring approach. These map directly to InfraRadar's nine alert categories.

**1. Political Risk**
Changes in government, policy, or political stability that affect the project. Examples: election of a new government that cancels its predecessor's contracts, nationalization of a private project, expropriation of land, change in concession terms, diplomatic breakdown affecting cross-border projects.

*Why it matters:* Political risk can cancel a project overnight regardless of its technical and financial soundness. Early signals include: election timing relative to project milestones, parliamentary debates about specific projects, protests near project sites, changes in ministry leadership.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Political news feeds, election calendars, government announcements, diplomatic signals. The alert surfaces as a political severity-graded notification on affected projects.

**2. Financial Risk**
Changes in the financial health of the project or its key participants. Examples: lender withdrawing from the financing package, sponsor running out of equity, currency depreciation making USD-denominated debt unserviceable, interest rate increases blowing up the financial model, cost overruns exhausting contingency reserves.

*Why it matters:* Financial distress is usually slow-moving and detectable in advance. A contractor that has been flagged in news as having cash flow problems will typically show signs 6-12 months before a project crisis.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Funding source changes, contractor financial news, currency volatility signals, MDB disbursement records.

**3. Regulatory Risk**
Changes in laws, permits, or regulatory requirements. Examples: environmental permit denial or revocation, land acquisition disputes leading to court injunctions, rezoning that blocks development, new environmental standards that require project redesign, customs duties on imported equipment.

*Why it matters:* A single permit denial can stop a $1B project. Regulatory risk is especially acute in countries with weak rule of law or where environmental movements are politically powerful.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Government gazette filings, regulatory body announcements, court filings, news on permit disputes.

**4. Supply Chain Risk**
Disruptions to the flow of materials, equipment, or skilled labor needed for construction. Examples: steel or cement price spikes, shipping route disruptions, equipment delivery delays (transformers, turbines), shortage of specialized labor, sanctions blocking access to critical components.

*Why it matters:* Supply chain disruptions are the primary cause of cost overruns and schedule delays that are not attributable to design changes. The 2021-2023 global supply chain crisis caused delays on virtually every major construction project globally.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Global commodity prices, shipping news, sanctions databases, equipment delivery lead time signals.

**5. Environmental Risk**
Environmental events or regulatory actions triggered by environmental factors. Examples: flooding, drought, or seismic activity affecting the construction site, discovery of protected species triggering a stop-work order, community displacement causing protests, tailings dam failure at a mining project, environmental litigation.

*Why it matters:* Environmental risks have grown significantly as ESG requirements from MDB lenders and institutional investors become more stringent. A project that fails its ESIA (Environmental and Social Impact Assessment) cannot proceed.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Environmental news, ESG filings, satellite imagery (land disturbance, flooding), community action news.

**6. Construction Risk**
Technical and execution risks specific to the physical build. Examples: geotechnical surprises (unexpected soil conditions, sinkholes), design errors discovered during construction, subcontractor insolvency, labor strikes on site, accidents that trigger stop-work orders, interface problems between packages causing delays.

*Why it matters:* Construction risk is the EPC contractor's core problem. They price it into their bids and try to transfer as much as possible to sub-contractors and insurers. If a project has a construction alert, EPC contractors and insurers are immediately interested.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Construction news, contractor announcements, site activity via satellite imagery, milestone tracking.

**7. Stakeholder Risk**
Risks arising from the behavior of key organizations involved in the project. Examples: main contractor filing for bankruptcy, key subcontractor defaulting, project owner changing strategy, joint venture partner withdrawing, dispute between project sponsors. This category is visible only to staff (researcher/admin) in InfraRadar because the data can be sensitive.

*Why it matters:* Stakeholder problems are often the proximate cause of project failure. Tracking the financial health of contractors in advance is a major value proposition - knowing a contractor is in trouble before the project they are building fails.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Contractor financial filings, news on corporate distress, JV announcements, ownership changes.

**8. Market Risk**
Changes in market conditions that affect the project's economics. Examples: commodity price collapse making a mining project unviable, power demand falling below forecast making a power plant unfinanceable, competing project being built nearby affecting market share, interest rate increases changing the cost of capital.

*Why it matters:* Market risk affects whether the project makes commercial sense regardless of how well it is executed. An investor tracking market conditions can identify projects at risk of cancellation before they are cancelled.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Commodity price feeds, sector demand data, competing project announcements, financing cost trends.

**9. Security Risk**
Physical security threats to the project, its workers, or its location. Examples: terrorist attacks targeting infrastructure, armed conflict in the project region, kidnapping of site personnel, piracy affecting a port project, civil unrest near the site.

*Why it matters:* Security risk is particularly acute in parts of West Africa, the Sahel, East Africa, and parts of South Asia. Insurance premiums, force majeure clauses, and evacuation plans are all driven by security risk assessments.

*InfraRadar monitors:* Security incident feeds, conflict zone databases, government travel advisories, local news.

### 1.8 The Intelligence Industry InfraRadar Is Replacing

Understanding the incumbent landscape is essential for sales.

**Traditional infrastructure intelligence products:**

*MEED (Middle East Economic Digest)*
A subscription service focused on MENA infrastructure. Provides project tracking databases, tender alerts, and weekly PDF briefings. Pricing: typically $5,000-$15,000/year. Major weakness: MENA-only focus, quarterly update cadence, no AI enrichment, no satellite verification.

*GlobalData*
A broad data and analytics firm with an infrastructure module. Provides project databases, market reports, and forecast data. Pricing: $10,000-$50,000/year depending on scope. Major weakness: expensive, static reports, no real-time signals, limited emerging market depth.

*Wood Mackenzie*
A premium energy and natural resources intelligence firm. Extremely detailed on energy projects. Pricing: $50,000-$200,000+/year. Major weakness: primarily energy-focused, very expensive, sold to large enterprises only, no agentic research, no satellite.

*Economist Intelligence Unit (EIU) / Oxford Economics*
Macroeconomic country risk analysis. Good for political and economic context but not project-level intelligence. Priced at enterprise level.

*In-house teams*
Many large EPC firms and DFIs maintain in-house research teams that manually track projects using spreadsheets, Google Alerts, government procurement portals, and periodic country visits. This is expensive (one analyst costs $80-150K/year in salary, not counting tools) and produces fragmented, unverified data.

**The gap InfraRadar fills:**
No existing product combines MDB pipeline data + real-time news + satellite verification + AI research agents + alert automation + portfolio tracking at a price point below $200/month. The market has a middle tier - between "free government portals" and "$50K/year enterprise databases" - that InfraRadar is designed to own.

### 1.9 Key Roles in Customer Organizations

When selling InfraRadar, you will encounter these roles:

**DFI / MDB Analyst**
Manages a portfolio of projects at an institution like World Bank, IFC, ADB, AfDB, EBRD, or a bilateral DFI. Tracks 50-300 projects across multiple countries and sectors. Their job is to monitor project implementation, flag problems early, and report to management. They have existing tools (institutional databases, manual trackers) but lack real-time alerting and independent verification.

Pain: Their information comes from borrowers (the governments or companies they lend to) who have an incentive to report progress favorably. InfraRadar provides independent verification.

**Business Development Director / Manager at an EPC contractor**
Responsible for identifying, qualifying, and winning construction contracts. Manages a pipeline of 20-50 active opportunities. Travels extensively in target markets. Pays for tender alert services but gets raw, unfiltered data.

Pain: They find out about tenders too late to prepare competitive bids. They have no early warning on which projects are real versus stalled. InfraRadar's planned-stage pipeline data and AI research agents give them 6-18 months of additional lead time.

**Project Finance Director / VP at an infrastructure fund or bank**
Structures and closes debt and equity packages for infrastructure projects. Manages deal flow, due diligence, and portfolio monitoring. Buys expensive research subscriptions but struggles with data currency.

Pain: The data they use for investment committee presentations is months old. Construction delays and distress signals emerge before they are officially reported. InfraRadar's real-time monitoring and risk scoring give them early warning.

**Infrastructure Analyst at an asset manager or PE fund**
Responsible for deal sourcing, market analysis, and portfolio company monitoring. Works under a portfolio manager or CIO. Creates the research materials used in investment decisions.

Pain: Emerging market coverage is patchy. They spend significant analyst hours producing what should be a query result. InfraRadar's research agent can produce a structured brief on any project in minutes.

**Insurance Underwriter - Political Risk and Construction**
Prices risk on large infrastructure projects. Needs project-level data to assess political risk, contractor track record, project stage, and evidence of construction progress.

Pain: They work with information provided by the insured party, which is inherently biased. Independent satellite verification is directly valuable.

**Government / SWF (Sovereign Wealth Fund) Official**
Tracks cross-sector infrastructure investment for national economic planning, or manages a sovereign fund's infrastructure allocation. Needs macro views, sector trends, and investment flow data.

Pain: No consolidated view across sectors and regions. No way to see what competitors (other countries) are doing.

### 2.3 Confidence Score

**What it is**
A number from 0 to 100 assigned to each project indicating how much verified, cross-referenced evidence supports the data in the project profile.

**How it works from first principles**
A project data point (say, "this project is currently in construction") can come from:
- A government announcement (single source, unverified)
- A news article quoting the government
- An MDB disbursement record (suggesting construction-phase loan drawdowns)
- Satellite imagery showing ground disturbance consistent with active construction
- A filing showing a construction contract signed with a specific EPC contractor

The more independent, corroborating sources, and the more recent they are, the higher the confidence score. A project with only a single government announcement from 18 months ago might have a confidence score of 25. A project with five independent sources including satellite verification might score 85.

**Why it matters in sales**
This is a key differentiator. Competitors give you data. InfraRadar tells you how much to trust that data. A DFI analyst managing a portfolio does not just need to know what is happening - they need to know how confident they should be in their information.

**The anomaly signal**
A project with a high claimed value but a low confidence score is flagged as an anomaly in the Risk & Anomaly Signals page. This pattern - big number, thin evidence - is a classic sign of a project that has been announced politically but has no real progress.

### 2.4 Risk Score

**What it is**
A composite score from 0 to 100 representing the overall risk level of a project. Derived from signals across all nine risk categories.

**Score interpretation**
- 0-24: Low risk. Project progressing normally with no significant flags.
- 25-49: Medium risk. Some signals of concern across one or more categories.
- 50-74: High risk. Multiple signals of concern; warrants active monitoring.
- 75+: Critical risk. Significant active flags; project may be at risk of delay, cost overrun, or cancellation.

**How it is generated**
The Risk Scorer AI agent runs every 4 hours. It reads the project's current data (stage, evidence, alerts, news sentiment, confidence score, stakeholder status) and produces a composite score. The score is not a simple average - it weights signals by severity and recency. A single critical alert can push a project into high risk regardless of its baseline score.

**Why this matters in conversations**
Portfolio-level risk views are something DFI analysts and investors currently produce manually using spreadsheets. InfraRadar produces a risk-ordered view of your entire tracked portfolio automatically. When the question in a meeting is "which projects in your portfolio should we be most worried about?" - that is the Risk & Anomaly Signals page.

### 2.5 Portfolio

**What it is**
The user's personalized tracked project list. Different from the Projects page (which shows all projects) - Portfolio shows only what the user has explicitly pinned for monitoring.

**What it does**
- Track up to N projects (scales with plan)

### 2.10 Alerts and Intelligence

**What it is**
InfraRadar's automated event monitoring system. The platform continuously monitors all tracked projects across nine risk categories and generates alerts when signals are detected.

**The nine alert categories in detail**

*Political* - election outcomes, government decisions, policy reversals, diplomatic events
*Financial* - funding structure changes, cost overrun signals, contractor distress, currency impacts
*Regulatory* - permit changes, court orders, compliance issues, new legislation affecting the project
*Supply Chain* - material price spikes, delivery delays, sanctions impacts, logistics disruptions
*Environmental* - environmental incidents, permit challenges, ESIA findings, community protests
*Construction* - milestone changes, progress anomalies, satellite-detected activity gaps, accidents
*Stakeholder* - contractor insolvency signals, JV changes, ownership changes (staff/researcher only)
*Market* - demand shifts, competing projects, commodity price impacts on project economics
*Security* - conflict, civil unrest, crime affecting project viability or personnel

**Severity levels**

- Medium (blue): Notable signal worth monitoring. Examples: minor schedule revision, new competing project announced, commodity price movement.
- Low (grey): Background information. Examples: routine news mention, minor regulatory update.

**Alert Rules**
Users can configure custom alert triggers: "Alert me with high severity any time a project in the energy sector in East Africa changes to 'At Risk' status." Rules run continuously against the live database.

**The Intelligence Brief**
Every four hours, the Alert Intelligence agent generates a narrative summary of recent alert patterns. It identifies: recurring patterns across multiple projects, regional hotspots (where alerts are concentrated), and actionable recommendations. This is not a list of alerts - it is an interpreted synthesis, as if a senior analyst read all the alerts and wrote you a paragraph.

**30-day alert history chart**
Shows alert volume over time with severity stacked. Useful for identifying periods of elevated risk and for reporting to management.

**Why this matters in conversations**

### 2.15 Evidence and Verification

**What it is**
The transparency layer that shows users exactly how InfraRadar knows what it knows.

**The five evidence types**

*Satellite*
Construction progress imagery from satellite analysis. Verifies that physical activity on the ground matches what is being reported. Satellite evidence is the most trusted because it is independent, cannot be falsified, and provides a ground-truth check on all other sources. A project claiming to be in construction that shows no ground disturbance on recent satellite imagery is an anomaly.

*Filing*
Official documents: government permits, construction contracts, environmental impact assessments, land acquisition records, corporate registry filings. Filings are high-credibility because they are legal documents with formal consequences for inaccuracy.

*News*
Media coverage from local, regional, and international outlets. News is useful for timeline and event tracking but lower credibility than filings because it can contain errors, be based on unverified government statements, or represent a single perspective.

*Registry*
Official government registries and databases: procurement portals, property registries, corporate registries, central bank filings. These are authoritative public records that are updated at government pace (slower than news, but official).

*Partner*
Data obtained from InfraRadar's data partnerships. These are third-party data providers who supply structured project data in exchange for commercial arrangements.

**The conflict detection feature**
A project is flagged as having a "conflict" when some evidence is verified and some is not - meaning sources disagree about the project's status. This is an important signal. A government announcement saying a project is "on schedule" combined with satellite imagery showing no activity and a news article reporting a contractor walkout is a conflict. The Evidence page surfaces these conflicts automatically.

**Stats on the evidence page**
- Total evidence items across the database
- Percentage verified
- Number of projects with satellite verification
- Number of projects with full coverage (4+ evidence types)
- Number of projects with conflicting evidence

**Why this matters in conversations**
Evidence and Verification is the feature that answers the question every institutional buyer asks: "How do I know this data is correct?" The answer is: you can see every source, their type, their date, and their verification status. This is radically more transparent than any competitor.
